Taming the Media Dragon

by Phil Groman

The introduction of free market competition in China’s media sector could be an early overture to the relaxation of Chinese press restrictions. Indeed, the process will be long and painful, but in a country notorious for government censorship and regulation, the emergence of media organisations that are driven by market forces and audience share is a considerable development.

One senior reporter from a major independent daily newspaper (who requested to remain anonymous) explained it thus: “In the government press, stories come straight from government agencies. There is no space to report on sensitive stories. But our survival depends on the market. If we report the same as the government we loose our edge – and people don’t like always hearing the government line.”

The Chinese media landscape can be broadly divided into two categories. The state run sector is controlled and funded directly by the government. Their content is largely dominated by party speeches, announcements and perspectives on events and policy making that are favourable towards the government.

The emerging independent sector began life as government subsidies dried up for certain of the state-run outlets. This forced existing newspapers and television stations to produce attractive programming in order to boost audience share and thus cover their costs through advertising. Currently, a new media venture is allowed to enter the market as a private enterprise, provided a local government agency is in support of the application. These new commercial ventures actively seek to feed the interests of their audience.

A good example of this development is the explosion since the early 90s in commercial talk radio. Traditionally the authorities would address the people through radio. However, the shift in regulation and audience demand has created a whole new lucrative media space that can be heard in almost every taxi in Beijing and Shanghai.

Indeed, a large part of the space is taken up by politically neutral celebrity and entertainment news. Yet, among the newspaper market there is a considerable shift towards investigative reporting and addressing politically sensitive stories.

Even with government regulation, increased access to information through the internet, a leap in literacy and growing scepticism towards the authorities has created an increasingly politically savvy media buying public. This market is forcing the industry to deliver politically sensitive stories. Hence, reporters are being encouraged to take risks and bold journalists will get bonuses for a well researched scoop.

One major business newspaper, The 21st Century Business Herald, runs a points system that ranks stories based on interest. The rating is designed to reflect the efforts that journalists take in finding and researching interesting stories. However, “sometimes the reporter will get paid but the story won’t be published because the final piece is too dangerous to make it onto the page,” said one Chinese journalist formerly at the Herald.

The country has a long way to go. China has consistently ranked at the bottom of Reporters Without Borders’ Worldwide Press Freedom Index and is considered to be the worst jailer of journalists in the world, according to the Committee to Protect Journalists.
Government regulators regularly issue directives on what can and cannot be published. During the 2008 Olympics a 21 point memo was issued to all media organisations instructing them on how to report the event. There is even a point about not reporting on food safety issues during the Games, which is believed to have contributed to the delay in the recall of Sanlu’s contaminated milk powder.

Recently, a database of journalists was announced that will track the actions of media professionals, with penalties imposed for those who break the rules.

These measures are the result of a paradigm shift in the media industry away from central government control. The authorities are on the defensive, forced to increasingly fight short-term fires with critical journalists, bloggers and global events. However, as the independent media sector matures the government will be forced to re-evaluate the resource required to maintain even partial control over China’s vast media landscape. With media consumption rapidly moving into the hands of the independents, a new harmony must emerge that will increasingly challenge the authorities into becoming more accountable.

A Young Middle East: America’s Golden Opportunity

by Jamil Wyne

The Obama Administration has indeed inherited substantial challenges in the Middle East that go beyond military and ideological obstacles: helping the region become more economically competitive; and garnering public opinions that will improve US-Middle East economic relations. While such macro-level topics will certainly command Washington’s attention, there also exists an often overlooked issue that both underlines the aforementioned challenges and affects America’s economic and political strategies in the Middle East: nearly one-fourth of the region’s 100-million youth (aged 15-29) population are unemployed. The Middle East is home to both the world’s largest youth population and highest youth unemployment rates. Expected to grow substantially for the foreseeable future, this young cohort will resoundingly affect the region’s future economic and political landscape, making it a demographic that deserves America’s consideration.

The Middle East’s ability to compete in the global economy will one day be determined by its youth. However, as long as this demographic remains without employment opportunities, the potential of the Middle East’s labour force will dwindle. Lacking efficacy in the global economy, the Middle East could fall into an extended economic downturn. Such circumstances would be exceedingly deleterious for youth, as they would further undermine their efforts to gain employment, in turn prolonging the region’s economic woes. It is therefore essential that the Middle East incorporates its young people into its work force so as to increase the region’s competitiveness within the global economy – a prospect that is of the utmost importance to America’s economic interests.

Furthermore, young people will also be instrumental in determining regional public opinions that will affect the Middle East’s policies toward increased integration into the global economy, which will have massive implications on the Middle East’s relationship with the US. With the rise of emerging Middle Eastern economies, the region has the potential to make increasingly advantageous contributions to the international economic playing field. And yet, without a favourable youth voice, effective policies towards such measures are hindered. Youth will serve as the linchpin in dictating public opinion toward such policies, and thus creating more avenues of employment could positively shape young people’s opinions. By identifying youth as key players in defining Middle Eastern public opinion the US could assist in directing the region down a more prosperous economic path, further evidencing the necessity for Washington to give Middle Eastern youth an increased degree of its attention.

If American policymakers ignore the considerable impact that Middle Eastern youth will have on the region’s relations with the US, they risk fracturing crucial economic and political ties with the Middle East. Setting a precedent of neglect could also weaken the chances of alleviating the conflicts in Iraq, Iran and Afghanistan and between Palestine and Israel. Alienating youth and their subsequent support could damage a solution to these challenges, which could plunge the region into even more dire circumstances. Consequently, assisting the region in providing economic opportunities to its young people is arguably one of the largest challenges facing US policymakers.

The formidable task of effectively addressing youth unemployment begins with examining a region-wide issue of quantity versus quality of education. While much of the Middle East is producing more university graduates, schools, and teachers, youth do not receive an education that prepares them to compete in the labour market. Given America’s advocacy of a liberal education that offers many of the skills Middle Eastern youth cannot easily obtain, assisting the Middle East in achieving increased prosperity will require increase exports of America’s education practices. Most Middle Eastern high schools and universities emphasize rote memorization and neglect writing and analysis skills, which employers highly demand. Additionally, the majority of young people believe that a university degree guarantees a public sector job, which further hinders their pursuit of employment as they prepare for university entrance exams instead of acquiring the much needed abilities mentioned above. Improved educational quality in the Middle East can act as both the catalyst for circumventing these obstacles, and the guarantor of economic stability. It is thus imperative that the US confronts this challenge with a nuanced understanding and approach to the underlying economic pressures the region’s youth face.

The youth plight is growing increasingly evident, and the consequences are sobering should they go unheeded. Recent polls from several research organizations suggest that the region’s young people are willing to play a more influential role in Middle Eastern society. If the US were to continue to create universities in the Middle East and increase idea sharing with the region, it would be supporting a promising demographic while laying a foundation for mutually beneficial economic and political relations. With youth morale high, circumstances are timely for the region and America to create more youth related policies and programs. While America’s challenge is to achieve a more holistic approach toward the Middle East, so is this timely opportunity.

The Pinnacle of Power

by Kevin Pearce

Recently the British bank Lloyds was fined $350 million for breaching US sanction laws for payments made to Iranian and Sudanese financial institutions. Further banks including Barclays and Credit Suisse are under similar investigations over similar allegations. Lloyds escaped a much larger fine by acknowledging its responsibility and fully cooperating with the OFAC lead investigation. This suggests that at the time Lloyds (and doubtlessly other banks) knew the risks involved in dealing with US sanctioned economies, but regarded themselves as not being submissive to US regulators. The fine to the contrary of this assumption in effect proves that non-US banks are effectively subject to economic sanction policies as defined by the US.

This sets a very interesting precedent; in effect European banks are subject to controls and restriction from US regulators. This example flows in the face of the recent upsurge in arguments questioning the strength and endurance of American financial prowess. With world trade currently in a state of freefall, largely due to a collapse in trade finance that funds it, what is needed is assertive and powerful American support for global trade.

However, questions of economic leadership are frequently levelled at the new US administration, lead by a man with a non-discernable record for economic policy. Obama’s views on NAFTA in the toughly contested Democratic leadership campaign did much to ruffle feathers across the 49th parallel and south of the Rio Grande. The new stimulus package with its clause to ‘buy American’ will leave many doubts as to the economic leadership credentials of the man of the moment. We can hope that Obama will do everything in his power to fight the creeping change towards protectionalist policies that will doubtlessly grow as voters worldwide seek familiarity in the midst of uncertainty.

Furthermore, Obama could use this proven American financial prowess in order to rectify, regulate and restrict the global financial imbalances that have caused the credit crunch. For much of the past decade East Asian nations have run huge trade surpluses that fuelled the vast quantities of foreign exchange reserves, predominantly saved in dollars and US T-bills. This has had a positive impact on American finances, essentially allowing the Federal Reserve to print money to fund the vast US military machine as well as a substantial trade deficit that has permitted an unsustainable consumer culture. With currency a commodity in no short supply, either in the US or the huge sovereign reserves of the Middle East the credit boom was an unsustainable and unregulated problem waiting to happen. What is needed now is for the Americans to put their enormous weight behind a global financial body, in a similar vein to those established at Breton Woods in 1945, that regulates and promotes global financial sustainability and stability. Current political overtures of national or regional solutions will not and cannot solve what is in essence a global problem.

Displacing the Domino Theory in Central Asia

by Robert Ledger

The “Domino Effect” theory was coined by the Eisenhower Administration in the United States in the 1950s. The theory’s label comes from the administration’s fear that successive countries would slide towards communism and they used the doctrine to justify covert action in Latin America and not so covert action in South East Asia. There is some evidence to suggest the theory held some weight. The bordering nations to Russia, China, North Korea, Vietnam, Laos and Cambodia all became socialist dictatorships while in Latin America Cuba, Guatemala, El Salvador and Nicaragua all went ‘red’ or had sizeable insurgencies.

In certain cases the United States’ opposition to communism caused an antagonistic and unintentional effect. Chasing the Vietcong into Cambodia escalated the civil conflict there and forced the Khymer Rouge forces into the centre of the country. The increased fighting culminated in the brutal Pol Pot regime that exterminated a quarter of the population from 1975-79 in an ultra communist programme. Despite the counter factual nature of the argument, this may not have occurred without US action in Vietnam and may have exacerbated the domino effect.

Now this is not particularly relevant today. If the big threat to the West (in terms of ideas and security) is religious fundamentalism not many predict or fear an Islamist domino as far as the nation state is concerned, although Jihadist groups are inspired by one another and germinate via the internet. Is there a parallel, however, in Afghanistan and Pakistan?

The circumstances are very different in so far as the societies effected, the global backdrop and the viability of the ideas at play. There are some similarities though. Both Vietnam and Afghanistan have resisted foreign occupation for decades. The battle for hearts and minds has been extremely difficult with resistant and surprisingly robust ideologies (communism and Islamic fundamentalism) hard to suppress. Both have neighbours that have been unstable for many years (Cambodia and Pakistan) and whose development has been neglected by the West. Both have seen violence spill over the border of the initial conflict into an already vulnerable area.

The result: what the US is trying to prevent in one state is actually displaced to the neighbour? The prospect of a nuclear armed country coming under an Islamist regime is not a happy one. President Obama has showed himself to have a much clearer grasp of contemporary history than his predecessor and would do well to consider the “Domino Effect” in pursuing stability in South Central Asia.

The Man Who Would Be King of Africa

by Corine Wood-Donnelly

In its rotating presidency, the African Union has elected Colonel Gaddafi as their new leader for the next year. The choice seems peculiar and it is possible that it signals Africa’s future alignment.

The African Union (AU) is intended to unify Africa and to promote its independence from former colonizers, improve development and infrastructure, and to align itself with the values upheld in the charter of the United Nations. Modelled on the structures of the UN, with peacekeeping forces, and mimicking the economic vision of the EU, the AU has been a positive, albeit not a completing, force for Africa. Thus far the AU has failed to impress.

As the AU has wished to attain legitimacy in the international community, the election of the authoritarian Gaddafi is a wonder. Since his rise to power several decades ago, Gaddafi has styled himself as the people’s dictator, maintaining power through various labels. He advocated a system that he labelled “Islamic Socialism” and has attempted to set up Libya under a system of direct democracy--rule by the masses.

In a commencement speech to Cambridge graduates in 2007, Gaddafi claimed that China will win the heart of Africa over its Western competitors because it is influencing the country through soft power. Given Gaddafi’s anti-Western history, it is likely that he will encourage the relationship between China and the AU in his role as president, undermining potential for the transferring of Western ideologies to the African people.

Perhaps Gaddafi, who has recently requested to be dubbed as the “king of kings,” will be able to unite the countries of Africa as a single state. However, it seems likely that that vision alone is not enough to set aside the destructive practices of Africa’s present. Should Gaddafi rid the continent of its many parasitic despots, perhaps he deserves to earn his royal title.

Perhaps for now, we can only hope that Gaddafi will be the strongman that will fulfil the AU’s mission, finally unifying Africa towards change and then surrender his AU presidency in a year’s time.

New EU Presidency: A Tough Mission

by Dariusz Drewniak

After six months in charge, Nicholas Sarkozy has handed the rotating presidency of the European Union over to the Czech president Vaclav Klaus, who will lead the Union till June.

Observers of political life in Europe look into the future with a dose of uncertainty, bearing in mind the somewhat Eurosceptical views of the Czech president who has recently compared the EU to the Soviet Union. In addition, the New Year also brings about quite a few international crises, including the Palestinian-Israeli conflict reaching a new critical stage.

As the first post-Soviet state to hold the EU presidency, the Czech Republic will face a challenging task: find a solution to the continuing Russia-Ukraine gas row that has already affected a number of EU countries. Since some of the states, most notably Slovakia, stand on the verge of a real energy disaster, the government in Prague will have to take firm steps to tackle the problem. It is also worth remembering that the Czechs are quite enthusiastic with regard to the American anti-missile shield being located in the Republic , as well as in Poland. In all probability, the pro-American attitude of Prague will not make negotiations with Russia any easier. Beyond this dilemma, however, there are more challenges awaiting Prague’s reaction.

During his last summit in December, President Sarkozy managed to form an EU agreement on the world’s climate change package, convincing Ireland to instigate a second referendum of the Lisbon Treaty, and he also reached an accord on Europe’s economic stimulus package of $258 billion. Implementation of these final agreements will now rest on the shoulders of the Czech Prime Minister Miroslav Toplanek, and will undoubtedly test the diplomatic and organizational capabilities of his ministers.

The EU presidency moving East coincides with Poland’s proposition of its Foreign Minister, Radek Sikorski, as the next secretary general of NATO, with the current holder of the position, Jaap de Hoop Sheffer, leaving the office in April. Such a scenario would significantly strengthen, albeit temporarily, the position of the Czech Republic and other central European states on the continent. It would certainly be warmly welcomed by the post-Soviet states, especially after the ‘lame’ reaction of the West to the recent Russia-Georgia war. The next six months will show whether the Czech’s management can take Europe to the next level on the path of European development.

When “Free” Trade Costs $700 Billion

by Rob Ledger

The current credit crunch and the role of the state in intervening in failing banks has brought us to a crossroads in economic policy.

Following the Depression of the 1930s and World War II, mixed economies and state intervention became the accepted (particularly in Western Europe) method for managing a nation’s finances. This system was challenged during the 1970s following successive crises exacerbated by oil price hikes. In the UK, industrial chaos and rampant inflation paved the way for Margaret Thatcher to come to power in 1979.

Inspired by the ideas of Milton Friedman and Friedrich Hayek (neoclassical economists in the tradition of Adam Smith), a new model came into fashion which was consolidated by the ‘Washington Consensus’ that has held primacy, up until now. Classical economists and New Right theorists that favour this approach consider inflation to always be a result of government interference. Markets should be left to find their own equilibrium while deregulation, private enterprise and ‘wealth creation’ are the order of the day.

Leaders enthusiastically talk about the ‘growth’ of their countries but in reality a few people have become very rich in the last 30 years while everyone else has not. Don’t get me wrong, I enjoy the benefits of the trickle down effect of this system. I write this on a budget laptop listening to my mp3 player.

Now we see banks collapsing and requiring rescue by our governments and our tax revenues. Where is free market capitalism now? Why is no-one defending a system that has become dogma? Where are the disciples of Friedman and Hayek to tell us that this is just a part of the capitalist cycle, that intervention will distort the market and induce inflation? Does it mean the end of neoliberalism, to be replaced by neo-Keynesian policies or just the start of a new set of boom and bust crises?

One of capitalism’s claimed strengths, was that it allows individuals to act in their own self-interest to the benefit of everyone else. It seems the human nature element is exactly what has brought on this mess…..greed…..to the detriment of everyone else.
